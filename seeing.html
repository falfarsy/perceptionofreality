The process of seeing

About a third of the brain’s cortex is engaged in vision, and when you open your eyes, billions of neurons and trillions of synapses are engaged. They are creating the object, shapes, colours and motion that we see in real-time to construct a reality. However, we don't construct the whole world at once, we construct what we need at the moment.

Occipital Lobe

The Occipital lobe has many important structures that enable vision:

The Brodmann Area (V1)

This structure serves as the brain’s primary visual cortex. The primary visual cortex is a structure essential to the conscious processing of visual stimuli. It aids the brain to determine spatial information and colour data.

The Ventral Stream (V2)

Information related to recognition to the temporal lobe, helping the brain assign meaning to what it’s seeing. Without V2 we would still be able to see, but we would not understand what our eyes took in.

Lateral Geniculate Bodies

All visual information from the retina stops here before being sent to the primary visual cortex.

Vision in real life

Vision provides a lot of information to the brain and in some cases, it can be overwhelming. For example, skier Mike May lost his vision at 3 years old due to a chemical explosion and recalls the incident as not seeming like a big deal. 40 years later, Mike underwent surgery to restore the physical damage done to his eyes. After the surgery, the overwhelming amount of visual information heavily confused his brain. All the visual information had no meaning or worth as the brain had developed without the chance to 'see' the world, so each piece of information had no value. He could not recognise his children as he had no face recognition whatsoever and proved to be a worse skier with eyesight than he was without as his depth and colour perception were off. This experience explains all the elements that have to be in place for the brain to construct a visual reality.

If from birth we weren’t able to interact with the world, in theory, we’d never be able to see. Our senses depend on each other and our reality is built by comparing the streams of data. These streams of data are then put together to create the current moment and many moments constructs a reality. However, if we block one sensory input from the brain it will still be able to construct a reality, however different. Furthermore, if we took away all sensory inputs, reality would still exist, just not to us.
