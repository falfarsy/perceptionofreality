Consciousness

Consciousness is the psychology of awareness. The term refers to your individual awareness of your unique thoughts, memories, feelings, sensations and environment. Your conscious experiences are constantly shifting and changing. For example, in one moment you may be focused on reading this sentence, then on the position you're sitting, then onto how excited you are to have your next meal. These shifting thoughts can change dramatically from one moment to the next, but your experience seems smooth and effortless.

What in the brain determines consciousness?

To distinguish between brain regions involved in reporting about a conscious perception from those involved in generating a conscious perception, we need to explain why activity in any region should be associated with consciousness. For that, we need a general theory of perception describing what the brain does in order for us to experience consciousness. In the 19th century, German polymath Hermann von Helmholtz proposed that a brain is a prediction machine. Everything that we see, hear and feel is nothing more than the brains best guess about the causes of sensory inputs. Sensory information from the body connects at the thalamus before going to the visual cortex, causing a heavy flow of information from the thalamus to the visual cortex. However, there is 6 times as much traffic flow in the opposite direction suggesting that at any one moment, what we experience as seeing relies less on the light streaming into our eyes and more on what's already inside our heads. Also, if we think about this logically, the brain has no direct access to the outside world. Instead, it receives ambiguous and noisy sensory signals that it must decipher. Perception must therefore be a process of interference in which sensory signals that are coming in through the body are compared with prior expectations about the way the world is. Together, this forms the brain’s optimal hypothesis of the causes of the sensory signals. To summarise, what we see is the brain’s best guess of what is out there.

How does perception occur?

Sensory signals enter through receptors such as the retina and as they progress deeper into the brain, each stage of processing becomes increasingly sophisticated. The classical view of perception is that the brain processes information in a bottom-up Processing information as it is coming in from your eyes outside-in direction. The Helmholtzian theory inverts this, proposing that signals flowing into the brain from the outside world only carry prediction errors - the difference between what the brain expects to see and what it receives. Therefore, using the Helmholtz theory, perception involves the minimization of prediction error within the brain’s sensory system by continuously updating the brain's predictions. In this view, perception is a controlled hallucination.

A number of experiments have shown that consciousness depends more on the brain’s predictions (top-down) Your brain applies what it knows and what it expects to perceive and fills in the blanks than prediction errors (bottom-up). In 2001, two Harvard medical students asked people to report what direction they sawing a cloud of drifting dots moving in. They used TMS A non-invasive form of brain stimulation in which a changing magnetic field is used to cause electric current at a specific area of the brain to specifically interrupt top-down signalling across the visual cortex and found that this erased conscious perception of the motion, despite the fact that bottom-up signals were left intact.

Where does this all come together?

In 1987, scientist Bernard Baars proposed the global workspace theory. He hypothesized that consciousness stems from a separated network of neurons that are widely spread throughout the cortex. This network receives information from separate sensory regions of the brain and puts it all together - filtering out and contradicting/unnecessary pieces of information to create a unified and smooth sense of reality.

An arguably more interesting theory is the integrated theory of consciousness (ITT for short), proposed by neuroscientist Giulio Tononi in 2004. According to this is theory, consciousness is linked to integrated information which can be represented by the mathematical quantity ‘phi’ (Φ). The part of the brain that supports our consciousness has high Φ and is therefore highly conscious. Parts with low Φ have little consciousness and parts with no Φ have no conscious.

What is meant by information?

The information that matters for consciousness is information a system has about it itself, which is measured by the number of possible states it could undergo. The brain consists of billions of neurons, allowing it to have an endless combination of neurons firing and not firing. However, knowledge of the current state of the brain cancels out most of the possible combinations; only a few combinations can cause the current combination and there are only a few combinations it, in turn, could cause. This is what gives the brain high information about itself, achieving IIT first requirement for consciousness.

What is meant by intergration?

IIT’s next requirement for consciousness is integration. Integration measures how much of the information of a system depends on the interconnections between a system’s parts. To determine this, the question ‘how much information is lost by cutting the system in two?’ is asked. In the brain, every neuron is connected to thousands of other neurons, forming intricate structures. If the brain were cut in two, a lot of this structure would be lost, along with any information that depends on it. This shows that it is a highly integrated system and that it’s information is not stored in chunks of the brain, but are instead interconnected.

However, having high Φ does not fully explain the brain’s consciousness. IIT’S third and final requirement is that a conscious system must be a maximum of Φ. This means, that it must there must be no overlapping systems that have a higher level of integrated information that the part that is responsible for consciousness, including other parts within the brain itself.

If ITT is correct, we would be able to measure the consciousness of any system by measuring its Φ. It would also solve a significant portion of the easy problem To understand how the brain (and body) stimulates perception, cognition, learning and behaviour , with the answer being only physical states with maximal Φ can be correlated with consciousness and simultaneously illuminate the hard problem To understand why and how any of the above should be associated with consciousness ie, why aren't we zombies? .
